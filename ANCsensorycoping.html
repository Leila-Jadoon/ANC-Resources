Sensory Coping and Coping with Stress
Sensory Coping
  1. Earplugs
  2. Headphones – besides wired headphones, there are also Bluetooth headphones that don’t require you to be tethered to your device. If you want to sleep wearing headphones or don’t like the feeling of earbuds in your ears, there are low-profile Bluetooth headbands.
  3. White noise/background noise generators
    1. MyNoise.net – Has tons of background noise generators for any mood that you’re in like rain, waterfall, cafe, forest, meditation garden, spaceship…
    2. Apps – There are tons of apps for Android, iOS, etc. which can help block out sounds. Search “white noise generator,” “background noise generator,” or “relaxing sounds” (Lisa uses Ambiance Nature Sounds by Mikdroid)
There is an app called SoundPrint that is used for crowd-sourced measurements of noise levels in restaurants and such like spaces.

Sensory-Friendly Spaces
  1. The music building. Contains soft, bouncy chairs. Good as long as one doesn’t mind background music (usually jazz combo rehearsal or someone’s voice lesson) that is sometimes present. Quietest places upstairs in the hallways--you can switch the lights off if you want.
  2. The Arboretum. Outdoors, so sound dissipates. Good in appropriate weather and if one doesn’t need electrical outlets.
  3. Courtyard behind Everson Hall (probably not a good spot now due to construction, but is extremely quiet otherwise). Outdoors, but there are benches/chairs inside if you want to be indoors. Oak tree in the middle provides shade along with other shorter shrubs.
  4. If you like cats, the Physics Building and Veihmeyer Hall are home to several kitties that are pretty friendly if you’re patient. There’s a calico kitty at Veihmeyer Hall that doesn’t like being petted, though (her bed has a sign saying she’d rather not be disturbed).
  5. If you enjoy nature, North Davis is home to a vast network of trails/greenbelts (pedestrian/bicycle-only roads--nice because you don’t have to worry about noisy cars), including some that go to a nice pond with lots of wildlife (geese (including baby goslings!), blackbirds, coots, finches/sparrows, wild turkeys, egrets, herons, hawks, kites, squirrels, dragonflies, ducks, and several barn owls). There’s a cat named Buster that likes to hang around the pond. These trails and the pond tend to be pretty popular among North Davis residents so you may have to experiment to find a time that won’t be too overwhelming. From personal experience, it’s usually quietest in the morning before 10 a.m. and in some afternoons.
  6. The Health Sciences District. This is in the southwest corner of campus (see map) and is very quiet because mostly grad students and nursing students hang out here. It is also very green and beautiful with lots of benches and outdoor tables. The Carlson Health Sciences Library is quieter and less busy than Shields. The Health Sciences District is a bit out of the way, but you can get there via the V or D Unitrans buses from the Silo or walk through Tercero and pass through the tunnel under La Rue.
